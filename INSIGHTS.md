# Cross-school insights from 44 universities

## 1. The "elite" tier is narrower than people think

Sub-7% admit rate in 2024-25 is now a tight cluster:

| Tier | Admit rate | Schools |
|---|---|---|
| Sub-5% | <5% | Caltech, Stanford, Harvard, Columbia, Yale, UChicago, Princeton, MIT |
| 5-7% | 5–7% | Northeastern, Penn, Dartmouth, Duke |
| 7-10% | 7–10% | Northwestern, Rice, Cornell, NYU, USC |
| 10-15% | 10–15% | Emory, Tufts, WashU, Georgia Tech |

The conventional "Ivy+ is selective" framing has shifted: the top 8 are
now genuinely a separate tier from everyone else, and Northeastern has
joined Penn and Dartmouth in the 5-6% range.

## 2. NYU and Michigan are application volume monsters

| School | 2024-25 applications |
|---|---:|
| Michigan | 196,620 |
| UC San Diego | 128,178 |
| NYU | 110,807 |
| Northeastern | 98,397 |
| UC Davis | 93,975 |

Michigan in particular is striking — almost double NYU's volume, while
maintaining a 15.6% admit rate. This is enabled by their large class
size (~7,000 freshmen).

## 3. Public flagships are still ~5x cheaper for in-state

| School | In-state tuition | Out-of-state tuition |
|---|---:|---:|
| Georgia Tech | $10,512 | ~$33,000 |
| UC San Diego | $14,934 | ~$48,000 |
| UC Davis | $13,602 | ~$47,000 |
| Pittsburgh | n/a | ~$22,000 |
| Florida | n/a | ~$28,000 |

Compare to private school tuition averaging $65,000–72,000. The financial
aid story complicates this — private schools meet 100% of need while
publics typically don't — but for full-pay families the spread is real.

## 4. Yield correlates with selectivity, but with notable exceptions

Yield ≥ 80% (high desire to attend if admitted): UChicago (88%), MIT (86%),
Harvard (84%), Stanford (82%).

Yield ≤ 25% (often cross-shopped, lots of admitted students go elsewhere):
UC schools (UC Davis 17%, UC San Diego 21%, UC Riverside 12%), Minnesota
(22%), Lehigh (28%), Brandeis (18%), Pittsburgh (13%).

The pattern: **low yield is dominated by public flagships and mid-tier
privates** that compete heavily with each other and with the elite tier.

## 5. The CDS publication choice is itself revealing

Of the schools we tried to ingest:
- ~33 publish PDFs reliably
- ~5-7 publish HTML only or in dashboards
- ~3-5 publish in Excel/XLSX
- A few (JHU, Williams, Notre Dame) block automated download

The schools with the most accessible, predictable URLs tend to be the
ones with strong IR offices and a culture of transparency (Stanford,
Princeton, Dartmouth, Cornell, Caltech). The ones that hide behind
dashboards or auth (Williams, Wisconsin, UT Austin) make replication
harder — which is presumably the point.

## What this can't tell you

The CDS reports averages, not distributions. It says nothing about:
- Net price by family income
- Outcomes after graduation
- Quality of teaching, advising, mental-health support
- The composition of the admitted class beyond demographics
- Anything specific to graduate or professional programs

For all those, IPEDS, College Scorecard, and survey data are better.
